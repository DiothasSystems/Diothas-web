The daily standup is a meeting where everyone reports state that a system already knows, to an audience that will forget it before lunch. It survives because it feels like coordination. It is mostly memory synchronization, performed at the speed of the slowest talker.

## What a decision actually is

A decision has four parts: who made it, what the alternatives were, why this one, and when to revisit. Almost no organization writes down more than the first. That's why the same argument resurfaces every eleven months with new participants and no memory of the last round.

Write the four parts. Give them a URL. Now a design doc can cite a decision instead of paraphrasing it, and a postmortem can point at the moment a tradeoff was made rather than reconstructing it from Slack archaeology.

> The test of a decision record is whether a new hire can disagree with it intelligently.

## Read-speed beats meet-speed

A written update takes ninety seconds to compose and ten to read. A standup takes fifteen minutes times the number of people in the room, and produces nothing durable. The asymmetry is not close, and it compounds every day.

The objection is always the same: writing is slower. It is slower to *produce* and dramatically faster to *consume*, and there are more consumers than producers. Optimize the side of the ledger with more entries on it.

## What to do on Monday

Kill one recurring meeting. Replace it with a written artifact that has an owner and an archive. Watch what happens to the questions people ask in the next one — they get sharper, because everyone arrived having already read the state.

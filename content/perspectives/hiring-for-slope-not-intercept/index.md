Every interview loop is a measurement of the intercept: what does this person know, right now, on a Tuesday, under artificial pressure. It is the easiest thing to measure and the least predictive thing you can know.

## The derivative is the signal

Two engineers with identical current ability, one of whom has been at that ability for four years and one of whom arrived there in eight months, are not the same hire. The second one will be unrecognizable in a year. The first one will be exactly where you found them.

Slope shows up in the stories people tell about being wrong. High-slope candidates describe a belief they held, what broke it, and what they believe now. Low-slope candidates describe a project.

## How to interview for it

Stop asking what they've built. Ask what they've changed their mind about, and listen for whether the answer has a mechanism. "I used to think microservices were always right, then I ran an on-call rotation for eleven of them" is slope. "I've grown a lot" is noise.

> Ask a candidate to teach you something they learned in the last six months. The quality of the teaching tells you the quality of the learning.

## The cost of the trade

Slope hiring is genuinely worse in the short run. You will ship less in the first quarter. You are buying a compounding asset with cash flow you'd rather have now, and that is exactly what makes it a real decision instead of a slogan.

Platforms are sold internally as leverage: build it once, every product team goes faster. The pitch is true and incomplete. What it omits is that every abstraction you introduce is a loan taken against future clarity, and the interest is paid by whoever debugs the system at 3am.

## The three taxes

The **cognitive tax** is the abstraction itself: a new engineer must now learn your platform in addition to the language and the domain. The **coupling tax** is that a platform bug is now everyone's bug. The **coordination tax** is that every product roadmap acquires a dependency on a team that does not report to it.

None of these are arguments against platforms. They are line items that belong in the business case and almost never appear there.

## The authority test

A platform team that cannot decline a request is not a platform team. It is a shared services pool, and it will be shredded by the loudest product manager in the company. The distinguishing feature of a real platform is a roadmap it owns and the standing to say *not this quarter*.

> If your platform team's backlog is the union of every product team's asks, you have outsourced your architecture to whoever files tickets fastest.

## Pricing it honestly

Before you build the platform, name the three teams that will use it and the specific thing each will stop doing. If you cannot, you are building infrastructure on speculation — which is sometimes right, and should be called what it is.
